## Type-setters' annual congress moves to Ashford

#### *Delegates from as far as Edinburgh expected; Lord Mayor to open proceedings Monday at the Guild-hall*

*By Margery Whitlock, Senior Correspondent*

**ASHFORD** — After a twelve-year residency in Sheffield, the Grand
Congress of the Amalgamated Society of Compositors has been quietly
relocated to this town for its forty-ninth annual sitting. Organisers
cited "unprecedented access to the river wharf" and "the general
civility of the inhabitants" as deciding factors, though the editor of
this paper suspects the matter was settled over supper at the
*Three Kings*.

Delegations are expected from Manchester, Leeds, Bristol, Dublin, and —
for the first time in eighteen years — Edinburgh. The Scottish party
will arrive by the Tuesday mail-coach, weather permitting.

<blockquote class="pullquote">
"The art is not yet dead, though the machine has had a good go at
burying it."
</blockquote>

Monday's opening session, held in the Guild-hall, will be addressed by
the Lord Mayor, followed by demonstrations of the new cylinder press
by Messrs. Harrowgate & Son. Tickets, priced at sixpence, remain
available from the Society's office on Cornmarket Street until
Saturday evening.

### Schedule of proceedings

| Day | Time | Event |
|---|---|---|
| Monday | 10:00 | Opening address, Lord Mayor |
| Monday | 14:00 | Demonstration — cylinder press |
| Tuesday | 09:00 | Debate: the future of the em-dash |
| Tuesday | 16:00 | Annual dinner, *Three Kings* |
| Wednesday | 10:00 | Papers, various |
| Wednesday | 15:00 | Closing remarks |

Visitors are reminded that no admittance is granted to the printing
floor without the showing of a member's card, a precaution made
necessary after the unfortunate incident at Leeds in '91.

> [!NOTE]

> by arrangement on Wednesday afternoon. Apply at the door.

## FEATURE · The art of the pull-quote

*By A. P. Hensley*

A well-set pull-quote is a small miracle of the trade. Too modest and
it passes the eye like a polite cough; too emphatic and it bellows
from the margin, drowning the matter it was meant to amplify. The
craft lies somewhere between the two, and like most things worth
doing, it resists explanation.

<blockquote class="pullquote">
A pull-quote is a doorway, not a lectern. It invites the reader in;
it does not harangue him from across the hall.
</blockquote>

The trouble with modern practice is that every journal seems to treat
the pull-quote as a species of advertisement. Great italic declarations
are set in display-sized type, walled off with ruled borders, and
colour-screened until the reader's eye alights on them by reflex
rather than by interest. This is an error of category. A pull-quote is
not a head-line and was never meant to be.

The older English practice — one still observable, in fragments, in
*The Strand* and *Cornhill* — was to set the lifted sentence in the
same face as the body, with only the italic and a modest rule to
signal its special status. Done this way, the pull-quote acts as a
landmark for the hurried reader: a place to pause, take in a phrase
entire, and decide whether to commit further attention.

### Three small rules

1. **Lift only a sentence that can stand alone.** A fragment dragged
   from mid-clause will dangle.
2. **Do not introduce it twice.** If the quoted line is about to
   appear in the body text a paragraph down, the reader has been
   offered the same pastry twice.
3. **Preserve the author's punctuation.** An em-dash truncated into a
   comma is an injury to the text.

## EDITORIAL · On the decline of the ampersand

It has been observed — correctly, we think — that the ampersand has
fallen from common use. Once a fixture of shop-fronts and invoice
books, the glyph now lingers only in the names of firms too
established to change their letterheads, and in the occasional
typographer's indulgence.

The loss is greater than it first appears. *Et*, compressed into a
single mark, saved a printer two sorts and a type-setter a moment's
attention. It was the very definition of efficiency, inherited from
the scribes of late antiquity and refined by the French writing
masters of the seventeenth century.

<blockquote class="pullquote">
Every glyph you retire is a sliver of memory you have chosen not to
keep.
</blockquote>

We do not propose a revival. The language moves on, and it would be
affected to insist otherwise. But we do ask this: when setting the
name of an old firm — Marshall & Sons, Pickering & Tucker, any of
the hundred dwindling partnerships on this county's high street —
reach for the ampersand and not the word. Every glyph you retire is a
sliver of memory you have chosen not to keep.

<blockquote>

nine to two, approved the purchase of a new Wharfedale cylinder press
for the town's official printing, replacing the forty-year-old hand

## LETTERS TO THE EDITOR

> **Sir,**
>
> I was distressed to read, in your issue of the 7th instant, a
> description of our Guild-hall as "charming." Charming is a word for
> cottages and puppies. The Guild-hall of this town was erected in
> 1612, has survived the Civil War, the Great Fire of '78, and the
> well-intentioned restoration of '71. It is not *charming*. It is
> *imposing*. Kindly instruct your staff.
>
> Yours, &c.,
> **Captain G. T. Pelham-Ward (ret.)**, The Crescent.

> **Sir,**
>
> Regarding the debate scheduled for Tuesday, I should like to put on
> record that the em-dash has no future worth discussing. It is the
> indulgence of authors who cannot bring a sentence to a clean full
> stop. A well-placed comma suffices for every occasion the em-dash
> arrogates to itself.
>
> I remain,
> **A. Lovett**, Compositor, Harrowgate & Son.

> **Sir,**
>
> Mr. Lovett's letter, published above — I write in the hope that it
> will be — wrongs the em-dash most grievously. The comma pauses; the
> em-dash *redirects*. They are not interchangeable, any more than a
> full stop is interchangeable with a semicolon. I trust Tuesday's
> debate will show this to the satisfaction of all present.
>
> Respectfully,
> **The Rev'd. H. Thornton**, St. Mary's.
